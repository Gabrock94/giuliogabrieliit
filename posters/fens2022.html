A non-invasive technique for recording the electrical activity of the human spinal cord

Gabrieli G., Lenoir C., Bufacchi R.J., Mouraux A ., Iannetti G.D.

The spinal cord functions primarily in the transmission of nerve signals from the motor cortex to the body, and from the afferent fibers of the sensory neurons to the sensory cortex. It is also the location of groups of spinal interneurons that make up the neural circuits known as central pattern generators. These circuits are responsible for controlling motor instructions for rhythmic movements such as walking (Guertin, P. A. ,2013).

The activity of the Spinal Cord is mainly studied in humans using invasive recording techniques (e.g. sub-dermal needles electrodes) or neuroimaging technique (e.g. fMRI). The aim of our project is developing a solution to non-invasively record the activity of the Spinal Cord using surface electrodes. Such a solution would solve common issues with fMRI recording, including but not limited to the costs of running an fMRI Scan, portability of the instrumentation, and variety of tasks subjects can perform.

The current study

In the current study, participants take part in three different tasks, arranged over two blocks.

The first task is a median nerve stimulation task. Stimulations are delivered at a rate of 4.75Hz, 1000 stimulations per block. In each block, a random number (5 to 30) of omissions is present. In total, our paradigm involves 8 blocks (4 on the left wrist, 4 on the right wrist). Of these, in 4 blocks (2 per side) participants are asked to count the number of omissions, while in the other 4 blocks they are asked to memorize a sequence of letters (Navon's task, Gerlach & Poirel, 2018).

Examples of Navons' letters.

In the second part of the session, participants are undergoing a (1) Cutaneous Stimulation Task, and a (2) Cutaneous Discrimination Task. In the Cutaneous Stimulation Task, participants' fingertips of both hands are simultaneously stimulated with sandpaper. The coarseness of the paper for the two hands may be identical or different. In the Cutaneous Discrimination Task, participants are given two spheres, one per hand, that may have the same or different diameter. Trails (N = 20) of the two conditions are intermingled (total 40 trials). Each trial lasts 10 seconds, with a 10 seconds inter-trial interval. At the end of each trial participants need to report whether the coarseness of the sandpapers or the diameter of the spheres were the same or different between the two hands.


Props used for the Cutaneous Stimulation and Cutaneous Discrimination tasks.

Setup

Simultaneous spinal (63 channels) and EEG activity (32 channels) are recorded (sampling rate 4096Hz). The ECG is also recorded.

All the signals are recorded using active electrodes (Biosemi ActiveTwo MK2)

During the session, participants are seated on a massage chair, with their neck extended and chest supported.

Pilot

A pilot (spinal only, 32 channels) was conducted on 3 subjects to verify the feasibility of the setup. Median Nerve Stimulation task was performed only on the left wrist. Cutaneous Stimulation Trials and Cutaneous Discrimination Trials were not intermingled.

Preliminary Results (Pilot)

Median Nerve Stimulation

Preliminary analysis identified a plausible physiological component ~10ms after stimulation. The component is stronger on the ipsilateral side, and appears to be earlier in time (~8/9 ms) on the spine at the level of the higher thoracic vertebrae, as compared to (~11/12 ms) lower cervical vertebrae.

Cutaneous Discrimination / Cutaneous Stimulation

Preliminary analysis suggest that a machine learning models (Multilayer Perceptron Classifier) is able to discriminate with high accuracy between trials of the two tasks, suggesting different physiological activity recorded at the spinal level according to the type of stimulation.

  2. H2: We can discriminate between signals recorded from two different conditions

Preliminary Results

Preliminary results confirmed the presence of the component identified in the pilot, as well as the differences in rise time at the different levels of the spinal cord.


Differences between Left and Right-side median nerve stimulation.

Electrodes Positions

The optimal position of the electrodes was empirically decided using a simulation software (Sim4Life). A traveling dipole was simulated in the spinal cord, moving from the thoracic (T5) to cervical (C3) vertebrae.
